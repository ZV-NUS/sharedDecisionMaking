## 1. Research Problem

**Confirmed from repository.** This project addresses human-machine shared decision-making and control for highway driving using highD-based data processing, learned driver-intent prediction, rule-based machine intent generation, bidirectional trust estimation, shared authority allocation, reinforcement-learning authority correction, and adaptive robust MPC-lite execution.

The implemented system receives road, ego-vehicle, surrounding-vehicle, driver-history, and risk-context information. It produces driver intent, machine intent, bidirectional trust, reference authority, RL-corrected authority, shared trajectory/control references, and final vehicle control commands. The target driving decisions are represented as left lane change, straight driving, and right lane change, while continuous control intent includes speed, acceleration, steering, and trajectory evolution.

**Writing suggestion.** The paper can define the research problem as follows: given a dynamic highway traffic state and predicted driver behavior, the system must allocate control authority between a human driver and an automated driving policy so that the final ego-vehicle behavior remains safe, efficient, stable, comfortable, and reasonably consistent with the driver's valid intent.

## 2. Existing Gap

**Confirmed from repository.** The implementation is built around several practical gaps observed during development and validation:

- Human intent prediction alone is insufficient because authority allocation and control execution require both high-level decisions and continuous control intent.
- A machine policy must reason about safety and efficiency even when the predicted human intent is unsafe, aggressive, or inconsistent with surrounding traffic.
- Reference authority generated by a fuzzy trust model may be too limited in hard cases, motivating an RL-based authority correction module.
- Shared authority must be converted into physically executable control commands through a controller that considers safety, stability, comfort, and tracking robustness.
- Fixed validation scenarios include cases where human intent is unreasonable, machine intent is efficiency-biased, human trajectory is too aggressive, adjacent-vehicle risk is high, or parallel driving creates safety risk.

**Writing suggestion.** The research gap should be stated conservatively: existing shared-driving pipelines often treat intent prediction, trust evaluation, authority allocation, and trajectory tracking as weakly coupled modules. This separation can produce unsafe or inefficient outcomes when human and machine intentions disagree or when the predicted driver trajectory is risky. The repository supports this gap as a motivation for an integrated prediction-trust-authority-control framework, but it does not contain a literature survey or external baseline comparison; those must be added separately.

## 3. Proposed Method

### Overall Framework

**Confirmed from repository.** The implemented framework contains four main work packages:

- **Work1: Human driver decision and control-intent prediction.** A Transformer encoder predicts current/future decision labels, future event direction and timing, future speed, and future steering from ego history, neighbor history, masks, and risk history.
- **Work2: Machine decision and control-intent generation.** A deterministic rule/artificial-potential-field policy generates machine decisions and continuous intent using front risk, TTC, headway, front-gap, relative-speed, and adjacent-lane safety checks.
- **Work3: Bidirectional trust and reference authority generation.** Trust from machine to human is computed from the rollout risk of predicted human intent. Trust from human to machine is computed from human-machine intent disagreement. An interval type-2 TSK fuzzy inference model maps trust and environment urgency into a reference human authority sequence.
- **Work4: RL authority optimization and adaptive robust MPC-lite control.** A Transformer-based actor-critic authority optimizer predicts an authority correction over the reference authority. The corrected authority fuses human and machine intent, and an adaptive robust MPC-lite controller tracks the fused reference under vehicle-dynamics, safety, stability, lane, comfort, and robustness costs.

### Inputs and Outputs

**Confirmed from repository.** Preprocessed highD samples contain:

- `ego_history`: ego state history.
- `neighbor_history` and `neighbor_mask`: six surrounding-vehicle slots and availability.
- `risk_history`: front-gap, headway, TTC, and risk-style features.
- `future_trajectory`, `future_speed`, `future_acceleration`, `future_steer`.
- `decision_label`, `future_decision_sequence`, lane-change count, return flag, and time-to-first-lane-change.

The final shared-control pipeline outputs:

- human decision/control intent,
- machine decision/control intent,
- bidirectional trust values,
- reference authority,
- RL-corrected authority,
- fused shared intent,
- final steering and acceleration commands from MPC-lite.

### Algorithmic Workflow

**Confirmed from repository.**

1. Preprocess highD trajectories into fixed-length history/future samples, including frame-wise decision labels and inverse-kinematics steering labels.
2. Train the Work1 Transformer driver-intent model on balanced HDF5 train/validation/test splits.
3. Generate Work2 machine intent using a rule/APF policy rather than a learned policy.
4. Roll out predicted human intent through a kinematic model and traffic-risk evaluator to compute machine-to-human trust.
5. Compute human-to-machine trust from human-machine disagreement over decision and control-intent predictions.
6. Infer reference authority using interval type-2 TSK fuzzy inference with a shared rule base across the prediction horizon.
7. Use the Transformer RL authority module to produce an incremental authority correction.
8. Fuse human and machine intentions according to the RL-corrected authority.
9. Track the fused reference trajectory and control references using adaptive robust MPC-lite.
10. Validate the complete pipeline in a highD-injected traffic environment and web-based visualization.

### Key Technical Mechanisms

**Confirmed from repository.**

- **Risk-aware intent representation:** Work1 uses ego history, neighbor history, neighbor masks, and risk history. The model has event-direction, event-time, speed, and steering heads.
- **Interpretable machine intent:** Work2 uses explicit rules for front urgency, overtake need, side safety, lane-change potential, future speed, acceleration, and steering generation.
- **Bidirectional trust:** Machine-to-human trust decreases with predicted human rollout risk. Human-to-machine trust decreases with human-machine intent disagreement.
- **Reference authority:** The interval type-2 TSK fuzzy model uses uncertain membership functions, rule consequents, environment urgency, and temporal smoothing.
- **RL correction:** The RL observation has 16 features per future step, including reference authority, trust, risk, urgency, front interaction features, human/machine speed, steering, acceleration, and decision disagreement. The actor outputs bounded authority corrections.
- **Adaptive robust MPC-lite:** The controller evaluates sampled steering/acceleration candidates around the fused reference and adapts objective weights based on risk, trust, stability margin, and authority.

## 4. Innovation Points

### Contribution 1: Risk-Aware Driver Decision and Control-Intent Prediction

**What is proposed.** A Transformer-based driver-intent prediction module jointly estimates discrete lane-change decision, future event direction, event timing, future speed, and future steering from highD-derived ego, neighbor, and risk histories.

**Limitation addressed.** A decision-only intent predictor cannot directly support shared authority and downstream control because it lacks continuous control intent and timing information.

**Technical difference.** The implemented model contains multiple prediction heads for decision labels, future decision sequences, event-level prediction, event timing, speed, and steering. The dataset construction also provides frame-wise intent labels and inverse-kinematics steering supervision.

**Benefit.** The module provides a richer human-intent representation for trust evaluation, authority allocation, and reference generation.

### Contribution 2: Bidirectional-Trust-Driven Shared Authority with RL Correction

**What is proposed.** A two-stage authority module first infers a reference authority sequence from bidirectional trust using interval type-2 TSK fuzzy inference, then refines it using a Transformer-based RL/actor-critic authority optimizer.

**Limitation addressed.** Fixed or single-sided authority allocation cannot capture cases where the human is unsafe, the machine is overly efficiency-seeking, or human-machine intent disagreement changes over the prediction horizon.

**Technical difference.** Machine-to-human trust is based on the traffic risk produced by executing predicted human intent, while human-to-machine trust is based on human-machine intent disagreement. The fuzzy model provides an interpretable reference authority, and the RL module learns bounded incremental corrections under safety, efficiency, comfort, smoothness, reference, and trust costs.

**Benefit.** The system can reduce reliance on risky human predictions or overly aggressive machine intent while retaining a smooth, interpretable authority prior.

### Contribution 3: Shared-Intent-Driven Adaptive Robust MPC-Lite Execution

**What is proposed.** A closed-loop execution layer converts RL-corrected shared authority into fused trajectory and control references, then uses adaptive robust MPC-lite to produce final steering and acceleration commands for the ego vehicle in a highD-injected traffic environment.

**Limitation addressed.** Authority allocation alone does not guarantee physically feasible, stable, or collision-free vehicle motion when predicted human intent contains errors or when surrounding traffic creates short-horizon conflicts.

**Technical difference.** The controller tracks fused human-machine references but adapts safety, stability, tracking, comfort, efficiency, and robustness weights according to risk, trust, stability margin, and authority.

**Benefit.** The method connects shared decision-making with executable vehicle control, enabling scenario-level validation of safety, stability, and efficiency behavior.

## 5. Experimental Setup

### Dataset and Splits

**Confirmed from repository.** The project uses highD-derived samples. The full all-frame-label manifest reports 11,485,341 samples across 60 shards. The packed HDF5 split summary reports:

| Split | Samples | L | S | R | Size |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Train | 1,076,922 | 201,008 | 717,948 | 157,966 | 18.91 GB |
| Val | 1,923,916 | 49,622 | 1,834,553 | 39,741 | 33.78 GB |
| Test | 1,219,824 | 40,015 | 1,142,355 | 37,454 | 21.43 GB |

The intent model uses a 3 s history window and 5 s prediction horizon at 25 Hz, corresponding to 75 historical frames and 125 future frames.

### Validation Scenarios

**Confirmed from repository.** The current shared-authority visualization export contains seven fixed highD-prototype plus controlled-environment-vehicle scenarios:

| Case | Scenario purpose | Expected behavior |
|---:|---|---|
| 1 | Lane-change intent is unreasonable because target-side risk is high. | Straight driving with deceleration. |
| 2 | Human intends left insertion into a risky adjacent gap; machine prefers the safer right side. | Right lane change. |
| 3 | Human left-lane-change direction is reasonable but predicted human trajectory is aggressive. | Left lane change with smoothed/safer trajectory. |
| 4 | Straight driving is inefficient because the front vehicle is slow/near. | Safe lane change for efficiency. |
| 5 | Straight intent is reasonable but deceleration is insufficient and adjacent lanes are blocked. | Stronger straight-line deceleration. |
| 6 | Parallel driving risk is high; machine acceleration to pass has excessive speed cost. | Follow human deceleration. |
| 7 | Parallel driving risk can be resolved because the adjacent vehicle is slower. | Accelerate to pass. |

**Important qualification.** These cases are not pure naturalistic highD scenes. The repository uses a highD prototype plus controlled surrounding-vehicle adjustments; the adjusted environment is fed into the algorithm and visualization.

### Metrics

### Training and Testing Settings

**Confirmed from repository.**

- Work1 Transformer: `d_model=64`, `nhead=4`, `num_layers=2`, `dropout=0.1`, `batch_size=128`, balanced train/eval sampling, six epochs, learning rate `3e-4`.
- RL authority module: observation dimension 16, future length 125, Transformer actor-critic with `d_model=64`, `nhead=4`, `num_layers=2`, maximum authority correction 0.35, eight epochs, hard-case/risk sampling enabled, and `oracle_supervision=true`.
- MPC-lite validation: horizon 12, wheelbase 2.7 m, ego car size 4.6 m by 1.8 m, maximum steering 0.45 rad, maximum acceleration 4.0 m/s^2, maximum steering rate 0.55 rad/s, safety distance 8 m, lane width 3.5 m.

## 8. Missing Information

The following information is still needed before writing a full IEEE Transactions manuscript:

- A formal literature review and reference list.
- External baseline methods for intent prediction, authority allocation, shared control, and MPC/control validation.
- Ablation studies isolating the contribution of risk history, machine intent, bidirectional trust, IT2-TSK authority, RL correction, and adaptive MPC weights.
- Repeated runs or multi-seed evaluation for the learned components.
- A full closed-loop evaluation over a larger test set, not only fixed scenarios.
- Clear separation between pure highD naturalistic scenes and highD-prototype scenes with controlled environment-vehicle adjustment.
- Table-ready metrics for controller stability, comfort, safety distance, lane keeping, and efficiency across many scenes.
- More robust evidence for cases 6 and 7, where current results remain collision-free but high-risk/low-clearance.
- A stronger explanation of the RL training regime because the current configuration uses oracle supervision; the paper should avoid claiming fully online RL unless additional training/evaluation is performed.
- A rigorous safety discussion for the MPC-lite controller, including whether constraints are hard, soft, sampled, or penalty-based.
- A final IEEE-style notation table defining all states, decisions, trust variables, authority variables, and control inputs.
